Still Deciding?

This is the most important decision of your life. Postponing it is itself a decision.

You’ve heard the gospel — you know what Christ has done. But maybe you’re hesitating. Maybe you’re not sure you’re ready, or you feel like you need to “get your life together first.”

The truth is, no one is ever ready in that sense. Salvation isn’t about you fixing yourself — it’s about admitting that you can’t, and trusting the One who can.

“Behold, now is the accepted time; behold, now is the day of salvation.” — 2 Corinthians 6:2

Every heartbeat is a gift of mercy. None of us are promised another day. To delay is to choose uncertainty when God offers assurance.

Why This Decision Can’t Wait

If a house is on fire, you don’t wait to walk out when it’s more convenient — you run. The gospel isn’t something to consider when life gets quiet or comfortable. It’s a message for now.

  • Life is uncertain. None of us know how long we have.
  • Sin is deadly. It separates us from the God who made us.
  • Christ’s offer is open. But it won’t last forever.

God’s grace is calling. The door is open — but it must be entered.

Common Questions

We all wrestle with doubts and questions. Tap to expand.

1) How do I know if I’m truly ready to be saved?

You are “ready” when you come to Christ as a sinner who needs Him, not when you’ve fixed yourself. In Scripture, those who were “pricked in their heart” (Acts 2:37) were told to repent and believe the gospel (Acts 2:38; Mark 1:15). Readiness is the work of God’s Spirit producing godly sorrow (2 Cor 7:10), a turning from sin to God, and faith in Christ’s finished work (Rom 3:24–26; 10:9–13). If you see your sin, believe that Jesus died and rose for you, and are willing to forsake self-rule to trust and follow Him (Luke 9:23; Isa 55:6–7; John 3:14–18), then come now—He says, “him that cometh to me I will in no wise cast out” (John 6:37, KJV).

2) What if I’ve done too much wrong for God to forgive me?

Christ saves “sinners; of whom I am chief,” said Paul (1 Tim 1:15–16). The blood of Jesus is sufficient for the worst of sinners: “though your sins be as scarlet, they shall be as white as snow” (Isa 1:18). At the cross, a condemned thief was pardoned that very day (Luke 23:39–43). God removes sin “as far as the east is from the west” (Ps 103:12) and casts it “into the depths of the sea” (Mic 7:18–19). Where sin abounded, grace did much more abound (Rom 5:20). The issue is not the greatness of your sin, but the greatness of Christ’s sacrifice—come to Him.

3) Do I have to change before coming to Jesus?

No. You come as you are; He changes whom He receives. Jesus came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance (Luke 5:31–32). We are justified by faith, not by self-reform (Rom 4:5; Titus 3:5). When you come, God gives a new heart and Spirit (Ezek 36:26–27), and then a new life flows out: “if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature” (2 Cor 5:17). The order is crucial: come to Christ → declared righteous in Him → begin to be made holy by Him.

4) How do I know this is real and not just emotion?

Build on God’s promises, not your feelings. Jesus promises to receive all who come (John 6:37); the gospel promises salvation to all who believe and confess Christ (Rom 10:9–11). God wants believers to know they have eternal life (1 John 5:11–13). The Spirit bears witness with our spirit (Rom 8:15–16), and over time fruits confirm reality: growing obedience (1 John 2:3), love for believers (1 John 3:14), perseverance in truth (1 John 2:19), and repentance when you sin (1 John 1:9). Assurance rests first on the objective work and word of God (Eph 2:8–9), then is strengthened by the Spirit’s ongoing work in you.

5) What if I fail again after becoming a Christian?

Christians still battle sin, but they do so with a Savior, an Advocate, and a new power. When you sin, confess—“he is faithful and just to forgive” (1 John 1:9). Christ is your Advocate with the Father, and His propitiation stands (1 John 2:1–2). God completes the work He begins (Phil 1:6); the righteous falls and rises again (Prov 24:16). Expect your Father’s loving discipline that yields holiness (Heb 12:5–11), and keep walking by the Spirit who enables real change (Gal 5:16–24). Your failures are not final because Christ’s righteousness and intercession are (Heb 7:25).

6) What if I don’t feel anything when I pray?

Seasons of dryness are common (Ps 42). We live by faith, not by sight (2 Cor 5:7). God invites you to pour out your heart (Ps 62:8) and to draw near with confidence because of Christ (Heb 10:19–22). Faith is fed by the Word (Rom 10:17), so stay under Scripture, prayer, fellowship, and the Lord’s Table (Acts 2:42; Heb 10:24–25). Examine yourself for unconfessed sin (Ps 66:18) and keep asking, seeking, knocking (Matt 7:7–11). Whether you feel it or not, Christ ever lives to intercede for you (Heb 7:25), and God meets those who wait for Him (Isa 64:4).

Counting the Cost

Jesus never hid the cost of following Him. He didn’t invite people to an easy life — He called them to die to self and live for Him.

“If any man will come after me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross daily, and follow me.” — Luke 9:23

Following Christ means:

  • Turning away from sin — not perfectly, but sincerely.
  • Placing Him above everything — even comfort and approval.
  • Trusting that He is worth infinitely more than what you leave behind.

The Christian life is not about adding religion; it’s about surrender — a new heart, a new master, a new purpose. It costs you your pride, but gives you peace. It costs you your sin, but gives you salvation.

The Cross Before You

“Whosoever shall call upon the name of the Lord shall be saved.” — Romans 10:13

You don’t need fancy words — only a sincere heart. God already knows what you need. If you’re still undecided, take time to read the Gospel again, pray honestly, and ask God to show you the truth.

But remember — neutrality is not an option. To remain undecided is to say “not yet” to the One who died for you.

“Choose you this day whom ye will serve.” — Joshua 24:15
